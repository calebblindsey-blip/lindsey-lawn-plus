When to Mow Your Lawn in Alabama — Season-by-Season Guide

Alabama Lawn Mowing Calendar

Alabama's warm climate means a long mowing season — but timing matters. Mow too early and you stress the grass. Mow too late in fall and you invite disease. Here's a month-by-month guide for North Alabama (USDA Zones 7b–8a), where most lawns are warm-season Bermuda, Zoysia, or Centipede grass.

Month-by-Month Guide

❄

January – February

Lawns are dormant. No mowing needed. Good time to schedule spring cleanup.

🌸

March

Grass begins waking up in late March. Watch for green-up. First mow typically late March or early April.

🌱

April – May

Growth accelerates. Start weekly or biweekly mowing. Edge and trim to redefine lines after winter.

☀

June – August

Peak growing season. Weekly mowing is ideal. Mow in the morning or evening to avoid heat stress on the grass.

🍂

September – October

Growth slows. Switch to biweekly mowing. Begin fall cleanup as leaves start dropping.

🌪

November – December

Final mow of the season. Focus on leaf removal and fall cleanup. Lawn enters dormancy.

Best Time of Day to Mow

In Alabama, the best time to mow is mid-morning (after dew dries, around 9–10 AM) or late afternoon (after peak heat, around 4–6 PM). Avoid mowing in the midday sun — it stresses both you and the grass. Never mow when the lawn is wet from rain or heavy dew.

Frequently Asked Questions

When should I start mowing in Alabama?

In North Alabama, most lawns need their first mow in late March or early April, once the grass has greened up and reached about 3–4 inches. Watch your specific lawn — the timing varies slightly year to year based on temperatures.

When should I stop mowing in fall?

Typically by mid-November in North Alabama. Once your grass stops growing (usually after the first sustained cool stretch in November), one final mow at the right height prepares the lawn for winter dormancy.

How often should I mow in summer?

Weekly is ideal for most Alabama lawns during June through August. Bermuda and Zoysia grow fast in the heat. Biweekly works but may result in removing too much grass height at once, which stresses the lawn.
